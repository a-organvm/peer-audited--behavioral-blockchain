# **1. Behavioral Economics Research (Commitment Contracts)**

Work from researchers like Katy Milkman and Richard Thaler consistently
shows that **financial stakes increase adherence**.

Key findings across studies:

- People are **2--3× more likely to follow through** when money is at
  risk.

- The stake must be **meaningful but not catastrophic**.

- Loss aversion is more powerful than reward.

  commitments               
  ----------------------------------------

The pattern:\
Deposits work best when they are **painful enough to matter but
affordable enough to join**.

For your audience demographic (mid-20s to late-30s women online),
research would suggest:

**\$50--\$150 deposit range is optimal.**

Below \$25 → no behavioral impact.\

# **Why It Could Be Seen as Gambling**

Regulators generally look for three elements when defining gambling:

1.  **Consideration** -- people pay money to participate

2.  **Chance** -- outcomes are based on randomness or uncertain events

3.  **Prize** -- participants can win money

If all three exist, something can legally be considered gambling.

Your model potentially includes:

- people paying money

- deposits being redistributed

- some participants "winning" money

So if the structure looks like:

> "Some people lose money and others win money"

it **can raise gambling concerns**.

However, the key legal difference is **chance vs. skill/control**.

# **Why Commitment Contracts Are Usually Legal**

Behavioral programs are typically allowed because:

- outcomes depend entirely on **participant behavior**

- there is **no randomness**

- participants control the result

This structure is commonly called a **commitment contract**.

A well-known example is the company StickK, founded by behavioral
economists at Yale University.

StickK allows users to:

- deposit money

- lose it if they fail

- direct where forfeited money goes

This model has operated legally for years.

The difference: **no prize pool framed as gambling winnings**.

# **The Risky Version of Your Model**

This structure creates the biggest regulatory risk:

Participants deposit money →\
some fail →\
remaining participants **win the pooled money**

That begins to look like a **prize pool**, which regulators sometimes
view as gambling-like.

Even though behavior determines the outcome.

# **The Safer Structures**

Most behavior platforms use one of these.

## **Option 1 -- Deposit Refund Model**

Participants deposit money.

If they succeed → they get it back.\
If they fail → they lose it.

Lost deposits go to:

- the company

- a charity

- a general fund

This is the **lowest legal risk**.

## **Option 2 -- Partial Redistribution**

Participants deposit money.

If they succeed → they receive:

- their deposit back

- a **small bonus funded by forfeits**

But the bonus is **not framed as a prize pool**.

Language matters a lot.

## **Option 3 -- No Redistribution**

Participants deposit money.

If they succeed → deposit returned.\
If they fail → deposit kept by company.

This removes most gambling risk but weakens motivation slightly.

# **What Legal Experts Usually Recommend**

For early-stage products, the safest structure is:

Entry fee = revenue\
Deposit = refundable stake

If someone fails:

- deposit forfeited

- company retains it

No prize pool.

Once the company grows, lawyers can structure more complex
redistribution models.
