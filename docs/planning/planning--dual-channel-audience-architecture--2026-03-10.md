# Dual-Channel Audience Architecture

This defines how Jessica and Styx should build audience without creating an empty-room problem.

The core principle is:

- `audience -> waitlist -> cohort -> ritual -> community`

Do not try to build broad community first. Build a room people want to enter because it already has a host, a reason, and the right first people inside it.

## Scope Lock

This architecture is for the current Phase 1 wedge only:

- `No-Contact recovery`
- `iOS` private beta
- `Test-Money Pilot`
- `US allowlist`

## Channel Model

### 1. Jessica Channel

Primary role:

- discovery
- trust
- emotional specificity
- founder / guide voice

What it should do:

- name the pain directly
- capture the live urge moment
- speak in human language about no-contact relapse, social media checking, late-night texting, and emotional backsliding
- bring people to the threshold of action

What it should not do:

- become a product brochure
- over-explain app mechanics
- sound corporate
- lead with financial or technical framing

Best CTA:

- `Join the Private Beta`
- `Get the no-contact guide`
- `Try the Do Not Text Your Ex Tonight tool`

### 2. Styx Channel

Primary role:

- product proof
- trust reinforcement
- scope clarity
- social proof

What it should do:

- explain what Styx is
- show what the beta is and is not
- surface product updates, screenshots, onboarding flow, beta milestones, user reactions, and trust assets
- convert interest into waitlist action

What it should not do:

- try to be the emotionally intimate primary creator voice
- compete with Jessica for personality-led attention
- over-index on abstract language like `behavioral blockchain`

Best CTA:

- `Apply for Beta`
- `See how it works`
- `Read the recovery guide`

### 3. Owned Channels

These are the real assets:

- waitlist
- email list
- qualified beta roster
- referral list

Role:

- convert borrowed attention into owned access

Rule:

- every public-facing channel must eventually route here

### 4. Cohort Layer

Primary role:

- make the first users feel like they are in a live room, not an abandoned system

Mechanics:

- small cohort admissions
- founder presence
- weekly rhythm
- milestone moments
- controlled referrals

Rule:

- do not open a broad community before cohort motion exists

## Role Split

### Jessica Arc

Jessica should have her own narrative arc.

That means:

- she is not only “promoting the app”
- she is building a trusted identity inside the no-contact niche
- her channel should still be valuable even when a post is not directly about Styx

Her content buckets:

1. pain naming
2. pattern interrupts
3. emotional truth / lived insight
4. founder/operator perspective
5. selective Styx invitations

What this does:

- preserves authenticity
- prevents the account from turning into ad inventory
- makes Styx feel like an extension of real insight, not a cold startup pitch

### Styx Arc

Styx needs its own account, but not as the first growth spearhead.

Its role is:

- product receipts
- proof of life
- trust and onboarding
- beta status
- safety and scope clarity

Its content buckets:

1. what Styx is
2. how the beta works
3. product screens and walkthroughs
4. trust/safety notes
5. early user proof

What this does:

- gives interested people somewhere credible to land
- lowers risk when they move from Jessica’s channel into a product decision

## Funnel Flow

### Jessica-led flow

1. Jessica post names the pain
2. Jessica CTA points to:
   `private beta waitlist` or `emergency page/tool`
3. landing page or tool captures email and qualification tags
4. accepted users move into cohort
5. active users later receive referral permission

### Styx-led flow

1. Styx post shows product proof or beta update
2. CTA points to:
   `apply for beta`
3. waitlist captures source
4. cohort manager decides admit / hold / nurture

### Practitioner / creator flow

1. outreach happens through direct contact
2. recipient sees one-pager or private beta page
3. interested partner takes a walkthrough
4. aligned partner becomes a source of qualified users

## Content Ratio

For the first 30-45 days:

### Jessica account

- `60%` niche-native value content
- `25%` personal/founder/progress content
- `15%` direct Styx conversion posts

### Styx account

- `40%` product clarity and proof
- `25%` trust/safety/scope content
- `20%` user reaction and milestone proof
- `15%` direct conversion asks

This keeps Jessica human-first and Styx product-first.

## Cadence

### Jessica

Weekly minimum:

- 3 to 5 short-form posts
- 2 story sequences or equivalent
- 1 direct CTA post
- 1 audience conversation prompt

### Styx

Weekly minimum:

- 2 to 3 product or proof posts
- 1 trust / safety / scope explainer
- 1 beta update
- 1 direct CTA post

### Email / waitlist

Weekly minimum:

- 1 waitlist update
- 1 cohort or milestone note

## CTA Discipline

Jessica should use:

- `Join the Private Beta`
- `Get the guide`
- `Try the emergency page`

Styx should use:

- `Apply for Beta`
- `See how it works`
- `Read the beta scope`

Do not make every CTA different. Repetition builds memory.

## The Empty-Room Prevention Rules

### Rule 1

Do not launch a broad Styx community before the first cohort has activity.

### Rule 2

Do not admit everyone. Admit enough people to create motion.

### Rule 3

Every admitted cohort needs ritual.

Examples:

- founder check-in
- weekly prompt
- milestone highlight
- selective referral invite

### Rule 4

Community should feel curated, not abandoned.

### Rule 5

Borrow attention first. Build owned community second.

## First Cohort Model

Suggested structure:

- cohort size: `15-30`
- source mix:
  - Jessica audience
  - creator referrals
  - practitioner referrals
- admission rule:
  - fit with no-contact wedge
  - likelihood of actual use
  - willingness to engage in feedback

What cohort 1 must produce:

- real onboarding friction notes
- live language from users
- first milestone proof
- first referral candidates

## Platform-Specific Reading

The current market pattern supports this architecture:

- creators build trust first, then invite people into deeper products or gated layers
- recommendations and referrals compound better than cold broad reach
- gated participation works better than empty open rooms in early-stage community products

## Operating Summary

Use this split:

- `Jessica` = attention, trust, emotional specificity
- `Styx` = product, proof, trust reinforcement
- `waitlist/email` = owned conversion layer
- `cohort` = first real room
- `community` = only after cohort motion exists

## Bottom Line

Do not try to make the app account carry the whole audience-building burden.

Jessica should be the human front door.
Styx should be the credible destination.
The waitlist should be the asset.
The cohort should be the first living room.
Only then should community become a real public-facing growth layer.
