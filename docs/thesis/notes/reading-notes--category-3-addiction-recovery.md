## 3.1 Contingency Management & Financial Incentives

### Volpp et al. (2009) — Financial Incentives for Smoking Cessation

**Key findings:**
- RCT: $750 incentive package → 14.7% cessation at 12 months vs. 5.0% control
- Nearly tripled long-term cessation rates
- Effect persisted 6 months after incentives ended (9.4% vs. 3.6%)
- Financial incentives effective even for low-income participants

**Styx application:**
- Strongest clinical validation that financial stakes produce real-world behavior change
- Key difference: Volpp used rewards (positive incentives); Styx uses loss aversion (negative incentives)
- Prospect theory predicts Styx's mechanism should be ~2× more powerful than Volpp's reward-based approach
- Persistence after incentive removal is encouraging for Styx's "habit transfer" hypothesis

**Dissertation use:**
- §2.4 as the landmark evidence for financial incentive efficacy
- §5.1 comparison: Styx's loss-averse mechanism vs. Volpp's reward mechanism
- §5.7 future work: Styx RCT should replicate Volpp's design with loss-framed incentives

### Stitzer & Petry (2018) — Contingency Management Review

**Key findings:**
- CM effect sizes: d = 0.46–0.58 across substance types
- CM remains underutilized despite strong evidence (only ~10% of treatment programs use it)
- Barriers: stigma, cost, "paying people to be good" perception, implementation complexity
- Prize-based CM (variable reinforcement) is more cost-effective than voucher-based CM

**Styx application:**
- Styx could democratize CM access by removing the clinical infrastructure barrier
- The "stigma" of paying for behavior change maps to Styx's Stygian terminology strategy
- Variable reinforcement: integrity score bonuses serve a similar function to prize-based CM
- Cost-effectiveness: user-funded stakes mean the platform doesn't bear incentive costs

### Benishek et al. (2014) — Prize-Based CM Meta-Analysis

**Key findings:**
- Effect sizes by substance: cannabis d = 0.81, cocaine d = 0.62, opiates d = 0.39
- Higher magnitude prizes → larger effects
- Longer treatment duration → better outcomes

**Styx application:**
- Differential efficacy by oath category: some behaviors will respond better to financial stakes than others
- Cannabis/cocaine high d-values suggest substance-related oath categories should see strong effects
- Aligns with tiered staking: higher stakes (higher "prize" equivalent) → larger effects

### Mantzari et al. (2015) — Financial Incentives for Health Behavior Meta-Analysis

**Key findings:**
- Significant effects on treatment attendance (g = 0.49) and medication adherence (g = 0.95)
- Effects on diet/exercise were smaller and less consistent
- Financial incentives more effective for discrete behaviors (attend appointment) than continuous behaviors (maintain exercise)

**Styx application:**
- Attendance-type oaths (check-in, show up) should have highest efficacy
- Continuous behavior oaths (maintain weight, sustain exercise) need stronger verification
- Proof submission cadence should be calibrated per oath type: daily for discrete, weekly for continuous

### Halpern et al. (2019) — Financial Incentives & Health Behavior Review

**Key findings:**
- Deposit contracts (user puts own money at risk) can be more effective than pure rewards
- But deposit contracts have low uptake (10-30% opt-in vs. 80%+ for rewards)
- Framing and defaults affect uptake: opt-out deposit contracts have higher participation
- Combining deposit contracts with social support increases effectiveness

**Styx application:**
- Low deposit contract uptake is Styx's conversion challenge — the 3.9% retention crisis includes an "opt-in" barrier
- Social support (Fury community, accountability partners) is evidence-based for boosting deposit contract efficacy
- The onboarding bonus ($5) partially addresses uptake by creating endowed-progress

## 3.2 Relapse Prevention & Recovery Models

### Marlatt & Donovan (2005) — Relapse Prevention

**Key findings:**
- Cognitive-behavioral model: high-risk situations → coping response → outcome
- Abstinence violation effect (AVE): a single lapse can trigger full relapse via guilt/shame spiral
- Lapse vs. relapse distinction: a single failure ≠ complete failure
- Covert antecedents: lifestyle imbalance, urges/cravings, cognitive distortions

**Styx application:**
- Grace days directly address AVE: 2 per month allow lapses without triggering contract failure
- The lapse/relapse distinction maps to Styx's proof rejection vs. contract failure
- Aegis protocol's downscaling after 3 failures prevents the financial spiral that mimics AVE
- Recovery stream oaths should explicitly frame missed check-ins as lapses, not relapses

### Larimer, Palmer & Marlatt (1999) — RP Overview

**Key findings:**
- Immediate determinants: high-risk situations, coping skills, self-efficacy, outcome expectancies
- Lifestyle factors: daily hassles, perceived indulgences, desire for immediate gratification
- Apparently irrelevant decisions (AIDs): seemingly harmless choices that increase relapse risk

**Styx application:**
- Notification timing should target high-risk windows (evening, weekends) per the immediate determinants model
- The "Grill-Me" AI feature can help identify AIDs (apparently irrelevant decisions)
- Recovery protocol's accountability partner requirement addresses the "social coping" dimension

## 3.3 Digital Therapeutics & Technology

### Boumparis et al. (2019) — Mobile Apps for Substance Use

**Key findings:**
- Only 6 of 20 reviewed apps showed significant substance-use reduction
- Most effective apps combined CBT content with monitoring
- Engagement was the primary predictor of effectiveness
- No apps used financial incentive mechanisms

**Styx application:**
- Financial stakes are the missing engagement mechanism that existing apps lack
- Styx fills a specific gap: apps have content but lack consequence density
- The combination of CBT-like features (Grill-Me, ELI5) with financial stakes is novel

### Gannon et al. (2023) — FDA Regulation of PDTs

**Key findings:**
- De Novo pathway used for most PDT clearances
- Evidence requirements: at least one RCT with clinically meaningful endpoints
- Predicate device comparisons available for some categories
- Post-market surveillance requirements for cleared PDTs

**Styx application:**
- Current positioning is consumer wellness, NOT prescription DTx
- Future clinical trajectory would require: IRB-approved RCT, clinically meaningful endpoints, De Novo application
- The 467-test prototype demonstrates technical rigor but not clinical evidence

## 3.4 No-Contact & Protective Order Psychology

### Cordier et al. (2021) — Protection Order Effectiveness

**Key findings:**
- Mixed evidence: violation rates range 7.1% to 81.3% across studies
- Legal orders alone are insufficient; enforcement and monitoring matter
- Compliance higher when orders are combined with other interventions

**Styx application:**
- Financial penalties fill the enforcement gap that legal orders lack
- No-contact contracts should be positioned as supplementary, not replacement for legal protection
- The max 3 targets and 30-day cap in Recovery Protocol prevent misuse

### Holt et al. (2003) — Protection Orders 18-Month Study

**Key findings:**
- Permanent protection orders: 80% reduction in police-reported violence
- Temporary orders less effective (suggesting commitment duration matters)
- Effectiveness varied by demographic and socioeconomic context

**Styx application:**
- Supports the evidence base for formal commitment structures reducing contact
- Duration matters: Styx's max 30-day cap forces re-evaluation (balancing commitment with autonomy)
- Demographic variation suggests Styx should not assume uniform efficacy across user populations

## Synthesis for Dissertation

### Key argument chain (§2.4 → §5.4):
1. Financial incentives work for behavior change (Volpp 2009, Mantzari 2015)
2. Deposit contracts (user's own money) can be more effective than rewards (Halpern 2019)
3. But deposit contracts have low uptake → Styx needs engagement mechanisms (social, AI coaching)
4. CM principles apply to consumer technology (Stitzer & Petry 2018)
5. Recovery applications require special safeguards (Marlatt 2005) → Aegis Protocol

### Safety argument chain (§5.4):
1. Relapse prevention theory: lapses ≠ relapses → grace days
2. Abstinence violation effect: failure cascades → Aegis downscaling after 3 failures
3. No-contact evidence: formal structures help but need enforcement → financial stakes
4. Duration caps: Holt (2003) shows temporary structures need renewal → 30-day Recovery cap
